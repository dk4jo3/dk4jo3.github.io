Solving Business Problem through Design

When the Epsilon People Cloud Customer (EPCC) platform faced critical challenges in usability, adoption, and user experience, I saw an opportunity to lead a transformation. With EPCC transitioning from a managed-service model to a self-serve SaaS platform, the stakes were high: we needed to reimagine its design to meet the demands of enterprise users and outperform competitors. This case study outlines how we tackled these challenges through a strategic, user-centered approach, laying the groundwork for a cohesive and impactful design evolution.

Act I: Setting the Stage

Solving Business Problems Through Design

The heart of design lies in problem-solving, and this project was no exception. The goal was to tackle critical challenges in Epsilon People Cloud Customer (EPCC), an enterprise-level Customer Data Platform (CDP) undergoing a significant transformation.

EPCC serves as a powerful platform designed to manage, structure, export, and analyze user data. Its target users—Data Architects, Data Scientists, Data Modelers, and Citizen Data Scientists—rely on its functionalities to make data-driven decisions. Traditionally an internal-only tool, EPCC was transitioning into a self-serve SaaS platform, a shift that demanded a complete rethinking of its design, workflows, and usability.

The Challenges

EPCC faced pressing business problems:

  • Difficulty moving beyond impressive demos to successful client adoption.
  • Growing pains from transitioning out of a managed-service model. There are some old artefacts of ‘bridge design’ from older cut plans of improvement. → inconsistent user experience + minor bugs here and there
  • Limited features and fragmented workflows that alienated users. → competitors have a lot more features

Act II: The Roadmap to Improvement

Strategic Plan: A Multifaceted Approach

Conducting an Audit: Collaborate with stakeholders to perform an audit of existing content, uncovering patterns of inconsistencies.

Defining the Core Principles: Establish the foundation by aligning with ZDS core principles and collaborating with marketing and branding teams to ensure consistency across the board.

What about AI?

Midway through the project, a new requirement emerged: adapting for AI features within the product. These interactions needed a distinct tone, different from the standard user interface voice. While conducting an inventory of AI touchpoints, I collaborated with stakeholders to define a specific tone that acknowledged the unique role of AI in the user experience. This led to the creation of a nuanced voice chart that addressed the needs of both AI and traditional content.

Navigating Resistance

Initial resistance came from the content team, who saw this initiative as encroaching on their domain. To ease tensions, I positioned the project as a starting point—a foundation they could build upon. By offering to hand off the effort once it was established, I fostered collaboration and mutual respect.

Act III: Delivering Impact and Looking Ahead

The Deliverable: A Living Document

The culmination of this effort was a comprehensive, adaptable document that became the gold standard for content across teams. While I handed off ownership to the main ZDS team, I continued collaborating with the content team to keep it updated and relevant.

Reflections on the Process: Lessons Learned

Without a concrete deadline, maintaining momentum was a challenge. To address this, I set up regular stakeholder check-ins to ensure steady progress. I also learned the importance of flexibility—our roadmap became more of a “living plan,” adapting to new priorities and feedback.

A Fulfillment Beyond Deliverables

Despite the challenges, the project was deeply rewarding. It allowed me to create something impactful for the organization while building cross-functional relationships and expanding my own skills. The process wasn’t just about improving the product; it was about fostering collaboration and learning to navigate complex dynamics.
