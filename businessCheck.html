1. Business & Stakes

The work began as a narrow request: “Can we drop a SQL-builder UI into the product?”However, discovery revealed a larger issue: hundreds of off-platform scripts were causing silent data errors and consuming support resources. I reframed the effort into a Centralized Business Check Repository that would surface, govern, and track every check, transforming a tooling request into a self-serve data-quality initiative.

Design Artefacts

To ensure the solution addressed user needs and was technically feasible, we designed an experience that balanced the flexibility of manual SQL queries with the usability of a traditional UI builder. Using these ideas, I created rough flows and visual artifacts to guide discussions with the broader team. I also wrote user stories to ensure alignment with the original goals and communicate effectively with PMs. This iterative approach helped us stay focused on creating a user-friendly and goal-oriented design.

Getting Buy-ins

As we refined the solution, we faced our next big challenge: gaining buy-in from the PPS team. Their role would shift significantly with this feature, and we couldn’t simply pitch it as “better because clients can use it.” Framing it as an enhancement, not a replacement, was key.
In collaboration with the PM, I positioned the feature to streamline their work, centralize processes, and reduce redundancy. Initial meetings were tough—we got some technical details wrong and faced questions about prioritization. The PPS team highlighted technical limitations, mismatches with underlying tech, and feasibility concerns.

Light at the End of the Tunnel

We quickly incorporated their feedback into the existing design and followed up with another meeting to discuss the updated approach. For elements that couldn’t be immediately integrated, we shared flowcharts and plans for future implementation, demonstrating how the design was scalable for expansion. Our goal was to show them that we were collaborative and took their feedback seriously while also articulating why this feature would significantly improve their workflow and make processes more self-serve-friendly.

Persistence and preparation paid off. The PPS team eventually embraced the idea and provided invaluable feedback on sequencing, transitions from spreadsheets, and reporting needs—insights that elevated the solution.

Act III: Navigating Trade-Offs and Next Steps

Diverging

With input from multidisciplinary conversations, we streamlined the scope to focus on the core goal: creating a centralized, cohesive experience for managing business checks. Some features were trimmed or re-prioritized:

  • Check Sequencing: Simplified for faster implementation.
  • Import Library: Included for flexibility.
  • Anomaly Detection: Deferred.
  • UI Builder: Pivoted to a simpler interface.
  • Reporting: Deprioritized but still documented for future use.

Bad News

As we worked on the reporting feature—exploring useful data points, grouping logic, and user perspectives—I received unexpected news: the entire feature was being deprioritized and tentatively slated for 2025 or beyond. It was disheartening, given the hours of collaboration, technical deep dives, and design iterations we’d invested.
But the process wasn’t a waste. Along the way, I built strong relationships with the PPS team, a group designers typically interact with only through PMs. I also gained a deep understanding of the data ETL processes in ZAIDYN, which will undoubtedly inform future projects.

Retrospective

What Now?

In the end, this case study isn’t just about designing a feature—it’s about learning to navigate complex systems, advocate for user needs, and find value in the journey, even when the destination shifts. Every step contributed to a foundation of trust, knowledge, and readiness for what’s next.

Lessons

Balancing Stakeholder Dynamics
Emphasize the importance of fostering a more balanced dynamic among stakeholders to avoid misalignment.

Pay closer attention to the onboarding experience Stakeholder Relationships
Reflect on the challenge of working with the PPS team, acknowledging both their expertise and the complexities they bring.

The Lesson on Fidelity
Highlight the value of using low-fidelity artifacts (like flowcharts) in early stages for clearer communication and faster iteration.
